# Brand Guidelines: The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints
**Global Visual Style Guide (GVSG) — Version 1.1**

> This document distills the essential design standards from the GVSG for use as a reference when building websites. Drop this file into any project and reference it when prompting the frontend-design skill.

---

## 1. Brand Identity & Purpose

**Who we are:** Christ's Church — men and women working on God's behalf to fulfill His eternal plan.

**Mission:** Help individuals and families enjoy the blessings of exaltation by sharing the gospel consistently and powerfully across all communications.

**Vision statement (use as design north star):**
> "All people can Discover God's Eternal Plan and Feel Inspired to Grow."

The four vision pillars — **Discover · God's Eternal Plan · Feel Inspired · Grow** — should act as "ingredients" when developing any communication. Design choices should invite exploration, connect content to divine purpose, create a desire for spiritual experience, and emphasize progression over perfection.

---

## 2. Visual Inspiration: Light

The central design metaphor is **light** — used literally and symbolically throughout all communications.

> "That which is of God is light; and he that receiveth light, and continueth in God, receiveth more light; and that light groweth brighter and brighter until the perfect day." — D&C 50:24

**Light as design language:**
- Colors softly shift to lighter, more luminous shades
- Transparent, layered shapes suggest radiance from a pure source
- Slanting rays and graduated opacity convey beams of light
- Warm, optimistic — never cold or harsh
- References the Light of the World: Jesus Christ

**Emotional qualities light should evoke:** Warm · Optimistic · Not complex · Hopeful · Natural · Discovering · Inspiring

## 3. Key Attributes (Brand Voice & Feeling)

Every design should be measured against these eight attributes. Not all will appear in every piece, but the emotional takeaway should be congruent with them.

| Attribute | Definition |
|-----------|------------|
| **Welcoming** | Inclusive, friendly, relatable. Confident and approachable — not self-righteous. We invite, never prescribe. |
| **Empathetic** | Aware of real needs and feelings. Meet people where they are. Both speaker and listener feel understood. |
| **Global** | Show we are alike as God's children. Include stories and images from all countries, communities, and cultures. |
| **Straightforward** | Clear, concise words and images. Avoid jargon. Do not impress, shame, or control. State truths honestly. |
| **Authentic & Honest** | Show life as it really is. Relatable voices — not showy or artificially ideal. Do not polish people's stories. |
| **Positive** | Share love, joy, and peace. Speak of difficulty with optimism and hope. Not excessively cheerful — confident. |
| **Humble** | Respect and dignity for all. Give credit to God. Acknowledge human failings and trust in God's perfect plan. |
| **Motivating** | Encourage personal growth. Give hope, invite action, inspire honorable behavior. Messages matter eternally. |

## 4. Typography

Only two typefaces are approved. Use them purposefully to control formality.

### McKay (Serif) — Primary
- A graceful serif font family with a classic, dignified style
- **Most commonly used** typeface in the identity
- Use for: formal communications, body text, headlines, scripture references
- Styles: McKay Regular, McKay Italic
- Formality signal: **McKay = more formal**
- All-caps McKay headlines convey the highest formality

### Zoram (Sans Serif) — Secondary
- A modern, graphic sans serif with contrast and legibility
- Adds informality and energy; replaces legacy use of Helam
- Styles: Zoram Semibold, Zoram Light, Zoram Extra Bold, Zoram Italic
- Use for: informal communications, social, educational materials, subheads
- Formality signal: **Zoram = less formal**
- Zoram used exclusively creates the most informal look; combined with McKay = congenial middle ground

### Typography Rules for Web
- **Web font substitutions:** Use a classic serif (e.g., `Georgia`, `Playfair Display`, or `Merriweather`) for McKay, and a clean geometric sans (e.g., `Inter`, `DM Sans`, or `Nunito`) for Zoram
- Limit font families to two — one serif, one sans serif
- Use weight and style variation (italic, semibold, light) to create hierarchy rather than adding more typefaces
- Body text: always in black or very dark gray for readability
- Never use decorative, display, or playful fonts

### Color Application Rules
- Use **monochromatic gradations** to naturally suggest light (lighter tints to darker shades of one hue)
- For **formal** communications: use deep, rich, timeless colors (dark blues, deep reds/magentas, dark greens)
- For **informal** communications: use bright, vibrant, friendly colors (light blues, yellows, bright greens)
- Avoid using multiple colors in a single light graphic treatment
- Always maintain at least 50% value difference between text color and background
- Black (`#000000`) is for body text; use Rich Black for large solid areas

## 6. The Light Graphic

The "light graphic" is a signature design element — a structured arrangement of geometric shapes that suggests slanting rays of light.

**Construction:** Four overlapping shapes — one horizontal rectangle and three triangles — all sharing a common origin point (typically top-left). Each shape uses white (or a palette color) at varying opacities to create a luminous gradient effect.

**Shape specifications:**
1. Rectangle: full width × narrow height, 60%–35% opacity gradient
2. Less transparent triangle: full width × ~30% height, 60%–25% opacity
3. More transparent triangle: 90% width × ~50% height, 35%–15% opacity
4. Reflecting triangle: full width × ~20% height, 60%–25% opacity

**CSS/Web implementation approach:**
```css
/* Simulate light graphic with CSS gradient overlay */
.light-graphic {
  background: linear-gradient(
    170deg,
    rgba(255,255,255,0.6) 0%,
    rgba(255,255,255,0.25) 40%,
    rgba(255,255,255,0.1) 70%,
    transparent 100%
  );
}
```

**Placement:**
- Headers, footers, hero sections, or app icons
- Not used on interior/body pages — primarily for "first views" and covers
- Never bisect a person's face with the light graphic origin
- Can be applied over solid colors or photographs
- Scale proportionally — never stretch horizontally or vertically

**Color in light graphic:**
- Primarily white at varying opacities
- Can substitute a single palette color (monochromatic) for warmth/mood
- Avoid multiple colors within one light graphic

## 7. Layout & Grid

### Print Grid
- 10 × 10 modular grid — all layouts built on this foundation
- Elements align to grid units for visual coherence

### Digital Grid (Responsive)
- **Mobile (phones):** 4 columns
- **Tablet:** 12 columns, 1–2 column layouts in portrait
- **Desktop:** 12 columns with full layout flexibility
- **TV / Projected:** 12 columns with 2-column margin on either side; no content in margins

### Digital Design Principles
- **Mobile-first strategy** — design for touch first, then scale up
- Navigation, links, and buttons must be easy to manipulate on touch screens
- Content density: loose on mobile, medium to dense on desktop
- Do not place content in the 2-column side margins on large/TV screens

## 8. Imagery & Photography

### Core Philosophy
Images should be **personal, candid, human, and authentic.** Research consistently shows this preference. Seek to capture truth, not manufacture it.

### DO
- Use both color and black-and-white images
- Capture members and moments "as is" — honest shots
- Suggest "doing" and convey an individual's story
- Capture context and environment — let surroundings tell the story
- Use names of people in images to add credibility
- Embrace diversity of age, ethnicity, location, and economic situation
- Use architectural and natural scenes that inspire or communicate a message
- Concentrate on people rather than the institution

### DO NOT
- Use posed or staged images; avoid casting models or prescribing wardrobes
- Artificially colorize or tint images
- Remove genuine flaws that make a person or place more authentic
- Use studio lighting or awkward poses as a primary style
- Use visual clichés or artificial emotion
- Use obviously outdated images unless age adds authenticity
- Use commercial stock images when original photography is possible

### Illustration
- Most flexible category — can convey broad concepts and metaphors
- Illustrations in a consistent style create visual connection across a series
- Select styles that are **simple and timeless**
- Pull from the approved color palette
- Do not mix multiple illustration styles within the same article or campaign
- Illustration style should correspond with the formality level of the piece

## 10. Writing Voice & Tone

The written voice should always reflect the key attributes: welcoming, empathetic, straightforward, authentic, positive, humble, and motivating.

### Tone Principles
- **Invite, don't prescribe.** Open front door; not a conveyor belt.
- **Meet people where they are.** Acknowledge their mindset and experience.
- **Speak of difficulty with hope.** Not dismissive, not excessively cheerful.
- **Be concise and clear.** Avoid unfamiliar jargon; explain unique concepts.
- **Be confident, not self-righteous.** State truths honestly and humbly.
- **Encourage growth, not perfection.** Progression is the message.
- **Give credit to God.** Humble attribution — not institutional pride.

### Language to Avoid
- Preachy, shame-based, or guilt-inducing language
- Artificially ideal or glossy descriptions
- Insider jargon without explanation
- Overly formal language in informal contexts

### Structural Voice Patterns
- Lead with the human or spiritual benefit, not the institution
- Use active voice and direct address ("you," "we")
- Short, declarative sentences for emphasis
- Longer sentences for nuance and empathy
- Scripture references as grounding (not as proof-texts)

## 11. Quick Reference: Design Decision Checklist

When building any web page or component, ask:

1. **Formality?** Who is the audience? What is the context? → Choose color range, typography weight, imagery style
2. **Light?** Where can the light metaphor appear? → Hero section gradient, color gradations from dark to light, transparency effects
3. **Color?** Is this formal (deep blues, rich reds) or informal (bright blues, yellows, greens)?
4. **Type?** Serif (McKay substitute) for formal/body, Sans-serif (Zoram substitute) for headings/informal
5. **Imagery?** Authentic and candid? Diverse? Context-rich? No staged or clichéd shots?
6. **Voice?** Welcoming, empathetic, positive, humble? No jargon? No shame?
7. **Grid?** 12-column responsive? Mobile-first? Content in columns, not full-width walls of text?

## 13. Component Design Notes

### Hero / Header Sections
- Use light graphic overlay on photograph or solid color background
- Light graphic originates from top-left or bottom edge
- Headline in serif (formal) or sans-serif (informal)
- Background color pulled from palette — blues for trust/spirituality, golds for warmth

### Navigation
- Clean, minimal — do not overload
- High contrast links (never under 50% value difference from background)
- Mobile: large touch targets; accessible

### Body Text
- Serif (`--font-serif`) for long-form reading
- Sans-serif (`--font-sans`) for UI labels, captions, navigation
- Black (`#000000`) on white/light backgrounds
- Comfortable line-height (1.6–1.8) for spiritual/reflective content

### Cards & Sections
- Use warm neutral backgrounds (`--color-neutral-lightest`) to create warmth
- Section dividers: subtle horizontal rules, not harsh borders
- Images always authentic and candid — never staged

### Buttons & CTAs
- Primary: `--color-blue-primary` background, white text
- Secondary: outlined in `--color-blue-primary`
- Informal/accent: bright color from palette (yellow, green)
- Rounded corners acceptable for informal contexts; sharper for formal
- Language: "Discover more" / "Learn more" / "Find peace" — never "Buy now" or aggressive CTAs

### Backgrounds
- Alternate between `--color-white` and `--color-neutral-lightest` for section rhythm
- Avoid pure black backgrounds unless high formality demands it
- Light-to-dark color gradients within a single hue suggest light naturally
